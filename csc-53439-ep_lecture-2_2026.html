Exploration and Uncertainty

Exploration-Exploitation Dilemma

A core Challenge in Reinforcement Learning


  • Agent must explore to discover rewarding actions and states.
  • Agent must exploit known information to maximize reward.
  • Balancing exploration and exploitation is critical for efficient learning.
  • Too much exploration: slow progress; too much exploitation: suboptimal policies.
Le dilemme exploration-exploitation est central en RL : il s’agit de trouver le bon compromis entre tester de nouvelles actions (exploration) et utiliser les connaissances acquises (exploitation) pour maximiser la récompense.
The exploration-exploitation dilemma is fundamental: agents must balance trying new actions to gather information and leveraging what they know to maximize returns. --- "without enough exploration, we cannot learn the environment well enough; without enough exploitation, we cannot complete our reward optimization task" "Different RL algorithms balance between exploration and exploitation in different ways. In MC methods, Q-learning or many on-policy algorithms, the exploration is commonly implemented by ε-greedy;" "With exploitation, we take advantage of the best option we know. With exploration, we take some risk to collect information about unknown options. The best long-term strategy may involve short-term sacrifices. For example, one exploration trial could be a total failure, but it warns us of not taking that action too often in the future." - https://lilianweng.github.io/posts/2018-02-19-rl-overview/#exploration-exploitation-dilemma - https://lilianweng.github.io/posts/2018-01-23-multi-armed-bandit/#exploitation-vs-exploration

Unstructured Exploration

ε-greedy

  • At each step,
    • with probability ε, select a random action;
    • with probability 1-ε, select the action with the highest estimated value.
  • Simple to implement, widely used in discrete action spaces.
  • ε is often decayed over time to favor exploitation after initial exploration.
Deep Q-Learning Algorithm
FOR EACH episode
$\quad$ $\mathbf{s} \leftarrow \text{env.reset}()$
$\quad$ DO
$\quad\quad$ $\mathbf{a} \leftarrow \color{red}{\epsilon\text{-greedy}(\mathbf{s}, \hat{Q}_{\mathbf{\omega_1}})}$
$\quad\quad$ $r, \mathbf{s'} \leftarrow \text{env.step}(\mathbf{a})$
$\quad\quad$ Store transition $(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{a}, r, \mathbf{s'})$ in $\mathcal{D}$
$\quad\quad$ If $\mathcal{D}$ contains "enough" transitions
$\quad\quad\quad$ Sample random batch of transitions $(\mathbf{s}_j, \mathbf{a}_j, r_j, \mathbf{s'}_j)$ from $\mathcal{D}$ with $j=1$ to $m$
$\quad\quad\quad$ For each $j$, set $y_j = \begin{cases} r_j & \text{for terminal } \mathbf{s'}_j\\ r_j + \gamma \max_{\mathbf{a}^\star} \hat{Q}_{\mathbf{\omega_{2}}} (\mathbf{s'}_j)_{\mathbf{a}^\star} & \text{for non-terminal } \mathbf{s'}_j \end{cases}$
$\quad\quad\quad$ Perform a gradient descent step on $\left( y_j - \hat{Q}_{\mathbf{\omega_1}}(\mathbf{s}_j)_{\mathbf{a}_j} \right)^2$ with respect to the weights $\mathbf{\omega_1}$
$\quad\quad\quad$ Every $\tau$ steps reset $\hat{Q}_{\mathbf{\omega_2}}$ to $\hat{Q}_{\mathbf{\omega_1}}$, i.e., set $\mathbf{\omega_2} \leftarrow \mathbf{\omega_1}$
$\quad\quad$ $\mathbf{s} \leftarrow \mathbf{s'}$
$\quad$ UNTIL $\mathbf{s}$ is final

RETURN $\mathbf{\omega_1}$

ε-greedy est la stratégie d’exploration la plus classique : elle garantit une exploration minimale, mais peut manquer de diversité et d’efficacité dans des environnements complexes.
Unstructured exploration uses simple stochasticity (randomness) to encourage the agent to try new actions. Effective in simple tasks, but often insufficient for complex environments. ε-greedy ensures some exploration but may revisit the same states/actions repeatedly and struggles in high-dimensional or sparse-reward settings.

Unstructured Exploration

Gaussian Noise

  • Add zero-mean Gaussian noise to the action output by the policy.
  • Common in algorithms for continuous control (e.g., DDPG, TD3).
  • Noise variance can be annealed to reduce exploration over time.
  • Encourages smooth, local exploration around the current policy.
  • Continuous Control With Deep Reinforcement Learning, Lillicrap et al., 2015.
    Algorithm: DDPG
$$\mu'(s_t) = \mu(s_t | \theta^{\mu}_t) + \mathcal{N}$$ where $\mu(s_t | \theta^{\mu}_t)$ is the actor network with weights $\theta^{\mu}_t$ and $\mathcal{N}$ is a noise process (e.g., Ornstein-Uhlenbeck).
Le bruit gaussien est adapté aux espaces d’actions continus : il permet une exploration locale, mais peut être insuffisant pour sortir de minima locaux ou explorer des régions éloignées. La méthode d’exploration est décrite dans la **Section 3 – Algorithm** du papier *Continuous Control with Deep Reinforcement Learning* (Lillicrap et al., 2016). --- Les auteurs expliquent qu’ils construisent une politique d’exploration (\mu') en ajoutant du bruit à la politique de l’acteur : où (\mathcal{N}) est un processus de bruit choisi en fonction de l’environnement. Plus précisément, ils utilisent un **processus d’Ornstein–Uhlenbeck** pour générer un bruit temporellement corrélé, adapté aux tâches de contrôle physique avec inertie.
Gaussian noise is effective for local exploration in continuous domains, but may not discover distant or rare states without additional mechanisms.

  • Unstructured methods (ε-greedy, Gaussian noise) struggle in environments with sparse rewards and complex exploration requirements.
  • Montezuma's Revenge (Atari): classic benchmark for hard exploration.
  • Random actions rarely reach rewarding states; agent fails to learn meaningful behavior.
  • Motivates the need for intrinsic motivation and structured exploration techniques.

Maximum Entropy RL

Exploration and Robustness via Entropy

  • Augments the reward with an entropy term to encourage diverse actions: $$ J(\pi) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi} \left[ \sum_t r_t + \alpha \mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot|s_t)) \right] $$ where $\mathcal{H}$ is the entropy and $\alpha$ controls the exploration-exploitation tradeoff.
  • Promotes stochastic policies, improving robustness and exploration.
  • Key algorithms:
    • Soft Q-Learning (SQL): incorporates entropy into Q-learning updates.
    • Soft Actor-Critic (SAC): off-policy actor-critic with entropy maximization, state-of-the-art for continuous control.
  • Widely used in robotics and continuous action domains.
  • Equivalence Between Policy Gradients and Soft Q-Learning, Schulman et al., 2017.
  • Soft Actor-Critic: Off-Policy Maximum Entropy Deep Reinforcement Learning with a Stochastic Actor, Haarnoja et al., 2018.
Le RL à entropie maximale ajoute un terme d’entropie à la récompense pour favoriser l’exploration et la robustesse. SQL et SAC sont les algorithmes phares de cette famille, particulièrement efficaces pour les tâches à espace d’actions continu.
Maximum-entropy RL augments the reward with an entropy term, encouraging stochastic policies and robust exploration. Soft Q-Learning and Soft Actor-Critic are leading algorithms in this area, especially effective for continuous control.

Parameter Noise-based Exploration

Noisy Nets, Parameter Space Noise

  • Noisy Nets: add trainable noise to network weights (Fortunato et al., 2018). https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.10295
  • Parameter Space Noise: perturb policy parameters directly (Plappert et al., 2017).
Le bruit paramétrique permet une exploration plus cohérente et structurée, car il modifie la politique de façon globale plutôt que d’ajouter du bruit à chaque action.
Parameter noise leads to more consistent exploration by perturbing the policy itself, often resulting in more effective discovery of new behaviors.

Intrinsic Reward-based Exploration

  • Augment environment reward $r_t$ with an intrinsic reward $r^i_t$: $$r_t \leftarrow r_t + \beta \cdot r^i_t$$
  • $\beta$ balances exploitation vs exploration.
  • Encourages agent to explore novel or uncertain states.
  • Two main approaches:
    • Count-based exploration: bonus for visiting rarely seen states.
    • Prediction-based exploration: bonus for high prediction error (novelty/surprise).
On ajoute une récompense intrinsèque à la récompense externe pour encourager l’exploration. Deux grandes familles : bonus de visite (count-based) et bonus d’erreur de prédiction (prediction-based).

One common approach to better exploration, hard-exploration problem, is to augment the environment reward with an additional bonus signal to encourage extra exploration. The policy is thus trained with a reward composed of two terms, \(\; r_t = r_t^{e} + \beta\, r_t^{i} \;\), where \(\beta\) is a hyperparameter adjusting the balance between exploitation and exploration.

  • \( r_t^{e} \) is an extrinsic reward from the environment at time \( t \), defined according to the task at hand.
  • \( r_t^{i} \) is an intrinsic exploration bonus at time \( t \).

This intrinsic reward is somewhat inspired by intrinsic motivation in psychology (Oudeyer & Kaplan, 2008). Exploration driven by curiosity might be an important way for children to grow and learn. In other words, exploratory activities should be rewarding intrinsically in the human mind to encourage such behavior. The intrinsic rewards could be correlated with curiosity, surprise, familiarity of the state, and many other factors.

Same ideas can be applied to RL algorithms. In the following sections, methods of bonus-based exploration rewards are roughly grouped into two categories:

  1. Discovery of novel states
  2. Improvement of the agent’s knowledge about the environment.
(src: https://lilianweng.github.io/posts/2020-06-07-exploration-drl/#intrinsic-rewards-as-exploration-bonuses)

Intrinsic Reward-based Exploration

Count-based Exploration

  • Assigns exploration bonus inversely proportional to state visit count: $$r^i_t = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N(s_t)}}$$
  • Effective in small, discrete state spaces.
  • Limitations:
    • Infeasible in high-dimensional or continuous spaces (counts become sparse or meaningless).
  • Alternatives: Density models approximate state visitation counts:
    • Bellemare et al., 2016 – CTS-based pseudocounts
    • Ostrovski et al., 2017 – PixelCNN-based pseudocounts
    • Tang et al., 2017 – Hash-based counts
Les méthodes count-based fonctionnent bien dans les petits espaces d’états, mais échouent dans les environnements complexes. Les modèles de densité permettent d’estimer la nouveauté dans des espaces continus ou visuels.

If we consider intrinsic rewards as rewarding conditions that surprise us, we need a way to measure whether a state is novel or appears often. One intuitive way is to count how many times a state has been encountered and to assign a bonus accordingly. The bonus guides the agent’s behavior to prefer rarely visited states to common states. This is known as the count-based exploration method.

Let \(N_{n}(s)\) be the empirical count function that tracks the real number of visits of a state \(s\) in the sequence of \(s_{1:n}\). Unfortunately, using \(N_{n}(s)\) for exploration directly is not practical, because most of the states would have \(N_{n}(s) = 0\), especially considering that the state space is often continuous or high-dimensional. We need an non-zero count for most states, even when they haven’t been seen before.

Intrinsic Reward-based Exploration

Prediction-based Exploration

  • Uses prediction error as intrinsic reward: $$r^i_t = \| f(s_t, a_t) - s_{t+1} \|$$ where $f$ is a learned forward dynamics model.
  • Agent is rewarded for visiting states where its model is uncertain or inaccurate (novelty/surprise).
  • Common in high-dimensional or continuous environments.
  • Limitations:
    • Model can be "fooled" by stochasticity or noise.
    • May focus on unpredictable but irrelevant states.
    • Motivates use of random networks (e.g., RND) for more robust novelty estimation.
L’exploration basée sur l’erreur de prédiction récompense l’agent pour la découverte d’états mal prédits par son modèle. Limites : bruit, imprévisibilité non informative. Les réseaux aléatoires (RND) sont une solution.

Learning a forward dynamics prediction model is a great way to approximate how much knowledge our model has obtained about the environment and the task MDPs. It captures an agent’s capability of predicting the consequence of its own behavior, \[ f : (s_t, a_t) \mapsto s_{t+1}. \] Such a model cannot be perfect (e.g., due to partial observation), the error \[ e(s_t, a_t) \;=\; \| f(s_t, a_t) - s_{t+1} \|^2_2 \] can be used for providing intrinsic exploration rewards. The higher the prediction error, the less familiar we are with that state. The faster the error rate drops, the more learning progress signals we acquire.

Intrinsic Reward-based Exploration

Focus on RND

  • RND uses two neural networks:
    • Target network: randomly initialized, fixed parameters.
    • Predictor network: trained to predict the target network's output for each state.
  • Intrinsic reward: prediction error (difference between predictor and target outputs).
  • States that are novel (rarely visited) yield high prediction error and thus high intrinsic reward.
  • As the agent visits a state more often, the predictor learns to match the target, reducing the intrinsic reward.
  • Encourages exploration of unfamiliar states.
Burda et al., 2018

RND repose sur la comparaison entre un réseau cible aléatoire (non entraîné) et un prédicteur entraîné à l’imiter. Plus un état est nouveau, plus l’erreur de prédiction est grande, ce qui génère une récompense intrinsèque élevée et pousse l’agent à explorer. À mesure que l’état est revisité, l’erreur diminue, réduisant la motivation à y retourner. Limits of Intrinsic Reward

Motivation for Memory-based Exploration

  • Intrinsic reward methods (e.g., novelty, prediction error) encourage exploration by rewarding new or surprising states.
  • Key limitations:
    • Slow function approximation: Neural networks may lag in updating, so states can appear "novel" longer than they are.
    • Non-stationary bonus: What is surprising changes as the agent learns, making the intrinsic reward unstable.
    • Knowledge fading: Once a state is no longer novel, the agent may forget its importance, even if it is crucial for solving the task.
  • Alternative: Memory-based exploration augments the agent with an explicit memory module to track visited states and contexts.
  • This enables more reliable novelty detection, stabilizes exploration signals, and helps retain knowledge of key states.
  • Example: Never Give Up (NGU) combines short-term episodic memory with long-term novelty signals (e.g., RND) to avoid forgetting useful states.
Les méthodes à récompense intrinsèque présentent trois limites majeures : lenteur de l’approximation par réseau de neurones (décalage entre la réalité et le signal), instabilité du bonus (non-stationnarité), et oubli de certains états importants (knowledge fading). Les approches à mémoire externe (memory-based exploration) permettent de stabiliser le signal d’exploration et de conserver la trace des états clés, comme dans Never Give Up (NGU).
Intrinsic reward approaches have key drawbacks: slow adaptation of neural approximators, non-stationary exploration bonuses, and forgetting of important states. Memory-based exploration methods address these by explicitly tracking visited states, providing more stable and persistent exploration signals (e.g., NGU). Les bonus d’exploration basés uniquement sur des récompenses intrinsèques ont des limites (instabilité, oubli, lenteur). Pour les corriger, certaines méthodes donnent à l’agent une **mémoire externe** qui lui permet de juger la nouveauté et l’importance des états de manière plus stable et durable. ### Contexte Dans le RL, beaucoup de méthodes d’exploration donnent à l’agent un **bonus de récompense intrinsèque** (par ex. basé sur la nouveauté ou l’erreur de prédiction). L’idée est : « si tu vois quelque chose de nouveau ou difficile à prédire, tu reçois un bonus, donc tu es encouragé à explorer davantage. » Mais ces **reward bonuses** posent plusieurs problèmes : ### Les trois inconvénients cités 1. **Function approximation is slow to catch up** * Dans le DRL, la fonction de valeur ou de bonus est approximée par un réseau de neurones. * Ce réseau met du temps à s’ajuster → un état peut rester considéré comme « nouveau » ou « surprenant » pendant plusieurs pas, même après avoir déjà été vu plusieurs fois. * Cela crée un décalage entre la réalité et le signal de bonus. 2. **Exploration bonus is non-stationary** * Comme l’agent apprend en permanence, ce qui est « surprenant » change sans cesse. * La fonction de récompense intrinsèque n’est donc pas stable : aujourd’hui un état donne un gros bonus, demain presque plus rien. * Cette instabilité complique l’apprentissage, car la politique poursuit un signal mouvant. 3. **Knowledge fading** * Au fur et à mesure que l’agent apprend, un état cesse de paraître « nouveau » et n’apporte plus de récompense intrinsèque. * Conséquence : l’agent peut oublier l’intérêt de certains états cruciaux pour résoudre la tâche (par exemple une clé ou un couloir important dans un jeu comme Montezuma’s Revenge), car ils ne donnent plus de bonus même s’ils sont essentiels. ### Alternative Les méthodes de **memory-based exploration** ajoutent un **module de mémoire externe** (parfois inspiré de la mémoire épisodique biologique). * Au lieu de se fier uniquement à un bonus instantané et évanescent, l’agent garde une trace explicite de **ce qu’il a vu** et **dans quel contexte**. * Cette mémoire permet : * de comparer un nouvel état à ceux déjà rencontrés (détection plus fiable de nouveauté), * de stabiliser le signal d’exploration (moins dépendant des fluctuations de l’approximation), * de rappeler à l’agent des états clés même s’ils ne sont plus « intrinsèquement nouveaux ». Exemple : dans **Never Give Up (NGU)**, l’agent combine une mémoire à court terme (dans l’épisode) et un signal de nouveauté à long terme (RND), ce qui lui évite d’oublier des états utiles. chatgpt.com/c/68dd976b-1440-8332-a0e8-7494f01f1fdb

Memory-based Exploration

Archive-based / Return-based Exploration

  • Go-Explore (Ecoffet et al., 2019): systematic exploration by explicitly archiving and revisiting states. [arXiv]  |  [Uber AI blog]
  • Two phases:
    • Exploration: Build an archive of visited states ("cells"), return to promising states, and explore from there.
    • Robustification: Learn robust policies to reliably reach and exploit discovered trajectories.
  • Highly effective in environments with sparse rewards (e.g., Montezuma's Revenge).
  • Distinct from intrinsic reward or parameter noise approaches: relies on explicit state memory and systematic return.
Go-Explore inaugure une nouvelle catégorie d’exploration structurée : l’agent archive explicitement les états visités, retourne à des états prometteurs, puis explore systématiquement à partir de là. Cette approche contourne les limites des méthodes basées sur la curiosité ou le bruit, et a permis des percées sur des environnements à récompense très rare comme Montezuma’s Revenge.
Go-Explore represents a new family of structured exploration: the agent explicitly archives visited states, returns to promising ones, and systematically explores from there. This approach overcomes the limitations of curiosity- or noise-based methods and has achieved breakthroughs in hard-exploration environments such as Montezuma’s Revenge.

Distributional RL

Categorical DQN (C51)

Introduction & Motivation

  • Classic DQN: learns only the expectation \(Q(s,a)=\mathbb{E}[R \mid s,a]\).
  • Lost information: variance, skewness, tail (risk) structure.
  • Distributional RL: model full return random variable \(Z(s,a)\) (not just its mean).
  • Distributional Bellman: \(\mathcal{T} Z(s,a) \stackrel{D}{=} R(s,a) + \gamma Z(S', A')\).
  • Implementation (C51): project updated distribution onto fixed atoms \(\{z_i\}_{i=0}^{50}\).
  • Benefits: richer training signal, more stable gradients, enables risk-sensitive objectives (e.g. CVaR).
  • Key idea: preserve probabilistic structure throughout the backup, then take \(\mathbb{E}[Z]\) only for action selection.
Dans cette partie, nous allons introduire la motivation derrière l’approche distributionnelle en apprentissage par renforcement, et en particulier l’algorithme Categorical DQN aussi connu sous le nom C51.

Commençons par rappeler que les algorithmes classiques comme DQN cherchent à approximer la valeur d’une action dans un état donné, c’est-à-dire l’espérance du retour futur.
Mais cette approche réduit toute l’incertitude, la variance, et la structure de risque à un simple nombre.
On perd donc toute information sur la dispersion des retours possibles, sur la probabilité d’obtenir un très bon ou un très mauvais résultat, ou encore sur la forme de la distribution (par exemple, si elle est très asymétrique).

C’est là qu’intervient le RL distributionnel.
Plutôt que de modéliser uniquement l’espérance, on cherche à approximer la distribution complète des retours possibles, notée Z(s,a).
Cela permet de capturer non seulement la moyenne, mais aussi la variance, les queues, et donc d’avoir une vision beaucoup plus riche de ce que l’agent peut espérer.

Mathématiquement, l’opérateur de Bellman devient distributionnel : il ne s’agit plus de propager une valeur scalaire, mais une loi de probabilité, qui est translatée et contractée à chaque étape.
L’algorithme C51 propose de représenter cette distribution par une discrétisation sur 51 points fixes, appelés « atomes », et de projeter la distribution mise à jour sur ce support à chaque itération.

Quels sont les bénéfices ?
D’abord, le signal d’apprentissage est beaucoup plus riche : au lieu d’un seul nombre, on supervise l’agent avec toute une distribution, ce qui améliore la stabilité et la vitesse de convergence.
Ensuite, cela ouvre la porte à des politiques sensibles au risque : on peut choisir d’optimiser non pas seulement la moyenne, mais par exemple la Value-at-Risk ou d’autres critères d’utilité.

Enfin, il est important de noter que même si la sélection d’action se fait encore via l’espérance de la distribution, toute la phase d’apprentissage bénéficie de cette structure probabiliste, ce qui explique les gains observés en pratique.

Pour résumer, passer de l’espérance à la distribution complète permet de capturer la richesse de l’incertitude, d’améliorer l’apprentissage, et de préparer le terrain pour des agents plus robustes et adaptatifs.
In this section, I’ll introduce the motivation behind distributional reinforcement learning, and specifically the C51 algorithm. Let’s start by recalling that classic algorithms like DQN aim to approximate the value of an action in a given state, that is, the expected future return. However, this approach collapses all uncertainty, variance, and risk structure into a single number. We lose all information about the spread of possible returns, the probability of very good or very bad outcomes, and the overall shape of the distribution—such as whether it’s skewed or has heavy tails. This is where distributional RL comes in. Instead of modeling only the expectation, we aim to approximate the full distribution of possible returns, denoted Z(s,a). This allows us to capture not just the mean, but also the variance, the tails, and thus to have a much richer picture of what the agent might experience. Mathematically, the Bellman operator becomes distributional: we no longer propagate a scalar value, but a probability law, which is shifted and contracted at each step. The C51 algorithm represents this distribution by discretizing it onto 51 fixed points, called “atoms,” and projects the updated distribution onto this support at every iteration. What are the benefits? First, the learning signal is much richer: instead of a single number, we supervise the agent with an entire distribution, which improves stability and convergence speed. Second, this opens the door to risk-sensitive policies: we can choose to optimize not just the mean, but also Value-at-Risk or other utility criteria. Finally, it’s important to note that even though action selection is still done via the expectation of the distribution, the entire learning phase benefits from this probabilistic structure, which explains the empirical gains observed. In summary, moving from expectation to the full distribution allows us to capture the richness of uncertainty, improve learning, and lay the groundwork for more robust and adaptive agents.

DQN Recap

Value-based Baseline

  • Goal: approximate \( Q(s,a) = \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t} \gamma^t r_t \mid s,a\right] \)
  • Network: input is state, output is vector \( Q(s,a) \) for all actions
  • Bellman backup (target): \( y = r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s',a') \)
  • Loss: mean squared error \( (y - Q(s,a))^2 \)
  • Limitation: collapses all uncertainty into a single scalar value
Souligner que DQN manipule seulement l'espérance: toute information sur dispersion et risque disparait.
Classic DQN uses scalar regression toward bootstrapped expectation; no distributional structure preserved.

Distributional RL

Key Idea

  • Random return: \( Z(s,a) \) is a random variable over \( \mathbb{R} \)
  • Distributional Bellman: \( \mathcal{T} Z(s,a) \stackrel{D}{=} r + \gamma Z(S', A') \), i.e., the law is transformed
  • Goal: approximate the law of \( Z(s,a) \) (not just \( \mathbb{E}[Z] \))
  • Expectation recoverable: \( Q(s,a) = \mathbb{E}[Z(s,a)] \)
Présenter la transformation distributionnelle: translation + mise à l'échelle par γ. Importance du choix de métrique (Wasserstein) et de la projection.
Emphasize distributional Bellman operator and theoretical contraction in Wasserstein metric.

C51

Categorical Approximation

  • Fixed discrete support: \( \{z_i\}_{i=0}^{N-1} \), with \( N = 51 \)
  • Bounded interval: \( [V_{\min}, V_{\max}] \)
  • \( \Delta z = \frac{V_{\max} - V_{\min}}{N-1} \)
  • Network predicts logits \( \to \) softmax \( \to \) \( p_i(s,a) \)
  • Approximate distribution: \( \mathbb{P}(Z(s,a) = z_i) = p_i \)
Choix fixe du support simplifie projection mais introduit rigidité si queue hors bornes.
Fixed categorical support enables simple cross-entropy optimization; clipping handles out-of-range targets.

C51

Bellman Update & Projection

  • Backup distribution: \( r + \gamma z_i \) (or \( r \) if terminal)
  • Clip to interval \([V_{\min}, V_{\max}]\)
  • Redistribute probability mass by linear interpolation to neighboring atoms
  • Projection operator \(\Phi\): maps shifted/scaled distribution onto fixed support
  • Approximately preserves expectation, stabilizes learning
Décrire interpolation: chaque Tz_i tombe entre z_l et z_u; masse pondérée selon distances. Référence Bellemare et al. 2017.
Explain linear redistribution of probability mass to adjacent atoms after shift/scale.

C51

Loss Function

  • Target: \( m' = \Phi \left( r + \gamma Z_{\theta^-}(s', a^*) \right) \)
  • Loss: \( \mathrm{CE}(m', p_\theta) = - \sum_{i} m'_i \log p_\theta(z_i \mid s, a) \)
  • Comparison: DQN uses MSE on a scalar value
  • Advantage: Multi-bin signal → more informative gradients
  • Empirical result: Improved stability on Atari benchmarks
Cross-entropy agit comme maximum likelihood sur distribution projetée; meilleure propagation d'information que MSE scalaire.
Cross-entropy between projected target distribution and predicted categorical distribution; richer supervision.

C51

Architecture

  • Backbone identical to DQN: convolutional layers followed by fully connected layers.
  • Output: $(\#\text{actions} \times N)$ logits, reshaped to $(\#\text{actions}, N)$.
  • Softmax over atoms for each action: $p_i(s,a)$.
  • Action selection: $a^* = \arg\max_a \sum_i p_i(s,a) z_i$.
  • Target: uses frozen (target) network and projection onto fixed support.
Souligner coût supplémentaire modeste: (N times plus de sorties) mais backprop similaire.
Implementation detail: flatten logits, apply grouped softmax; expectation used for policy.

C51

Atari Results (Summary)

  • Improved median score compared to vanilla DQN
  • Faster convergence in the first millions of frames
  • Reduced training variance
  • Foundation for Rainbow (integration of further improvements)
Citer Bellemare et al. 2017: gains systématiques sur benchmarks Atari; distributional perspective bénéfique seule.
Empirical gains largely attributed to richer target signal and improved optimization landscape.

C51

Limitations & Extensions

  • Fixed support: risk of saturation if returns fall outside $[V_{\min}, V_{\max}]$
  • Uniform resolution: inefficient for modeling sharp tails or skewed distributions
  • Extensions:
    • QR-DQN: learns quantiles, reducing projection bias
    • IQN: continuous implicit quantile function for flexible modeling
    • FQF: learnable fraction proposal for adaptive quantile allocation
  • Paradigm: working with distributions enables risk-sensitive and utility-based RL
Transition naturelle vers méthodes quantiles pour adaptativité; mentionner FQF pour allocation dynamique des quantiles.
Limitations of fixed categorical grid motivate quantile-based and adaptive approaches.

C51

Conclusion & Discussion

  • Moving from expectation to distribution provides a richer learning signal.
  • C51 is simple, effective, and forms the basis for many improvements.
  • Actions are selected via the expectation: the full distribution is valuable for training and for future risk-sensitive objectives.
  • Enables risk-sensitive RL, robustness, and informed exploration.
  • Open questions: how to choose the support? How to combine with epistemic uncertainty?
Répondre à: pourquoi modéliser distribution si on agit via espérance? → gradients plus informatifs, possibilité postérieure d'utiliser risque/utility sans réentraîner.
Even if acting greedily on expectation, learning benefits from structured multi-target supervision; distribution reusable for risk measures.

Probability-based Algorithms

C51, Categorical DQN

Input:
$\quad\quad$ none
Algorithm parameters:
$\quad\quad$ discount factor $\gamma$
$\quad\quad$ learning rate $\alpha \in (0,1]$
$\quad\quad$ small $\epsilon > 0$
$\quad\quad$ capacity of the replay memory $M$
$\quad\quad$ batch size $m$
$\quad\quad$ target network update period $C$
$\quad\quad$ number of atoms $N$
$\quad\quad$ value range $[V_{\min}, V_{\max}]$
$\quad\quad$ fixed support $z_i = V_{\min} + i \Delta z,\; i=0,\dots,N-1$ with $\Delta z = \frac{V_{\max}-V_{\min}}{N-1}$

Initialize replay memory $\mathcal{D}$ to capacity $M$
Initialize action-value distribution network $\hat{Z}_{\mathbf{\omega_1}}$ with random weights $\mathbf{\omega_1}$
Initialize target distribution network $\hat{Z}_{\mathbf{\omega_2}}$ with weights $\mathbf{\omega_2} \leftarrow \mathbf{\omega_1}$

FOR EACH episode
$\quad$ $\mathbf{s} \leftarrow \text{env.reset}()$
$\quad$ DO
$\quad\quad$ $\mathbf{a} \leftarrow \epsilon\text{-greedy}(\mathbf{s}, \hat{Z}_{\mathbf{\omega_1}})$ $\quad$ // choose action by maximizing $\mathbb{E}[Z_{\omega_1}(s,a)]$
$\quad\quad$ $r, \mathbf{s'} \leftarrow \text{env.step}(\mathbf{a})$
$\quad\quad$ Store transition $(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{a}, r, \mathbf{s'})$ in $\mathcal{D}$
$\quad\quad$ If $\mathcal{D}$ contains at least $m$ transitions
$\quad\quad\quad$ Sample random batch $(\mathbf{s}_j, \mathbf{a}_j, r_j, \mathbf{s'}_j)$ with $j=1$ to $m$
$\quad\quad\quad$ For each $j$:
$\quad\quad\quad\quad$ If $\mathbf{s'}_j$ is terminal: set $Tz = \min\big(V_{\max}, \max(V_{\min}, r_j)\big)$
$\quad\quad\quad\quad$ Else: $Tz_i = \min\big(V_{\max}, \max(V_{\min}, r_j + \gamma z_i)\big),\;\; i=0,\dots,N-1$
$\quad\quad\quad\quad$ Compute greedy action $\mathbf{a}^\star = \arg\max_{\mathbf{a}} \sum_i z_i \cdot p_{\omega_2}(z_i \mid \mathbf{s'}_j, \mathbf{a})$
$\quad\quad\quad\quad$ Construct target distribution by **projecting** shifted support $\{Tz_i\}$ onto fixed atoms $\{z_i\}$: $\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad$ $m^{(j)} \leftarrow \Phi_{\mathbf{s'}_j,\mathbf{a}^\star}[r_j, \gamma, p_{\omega_2}]$
$\quad\quad\quad$ Perform a gradient descent step to minimize the cross-entropy: $\quad\quad\quad\quad$ $L = -\sum_{i=0}^{N-1} m^{(j)}_i \log p_{\omega_1}(z_i \mid \mathbf{s}_j, \mathbf{a}_j)$
$\quad\quad\quad$ Every $C$ steps, update target: $\mathbf{\omega_2} \leftarrow \mathbf{\omega_1}$
$\quad\quad$ $\mathbf{s} \leftarrow \mathbf{s'}$
$\quad$ UNTIL $\mathbf{s}$ is terminal

RETURN $\mathbf{\omega_1}$ (i.e., the learned distributional action-value function $Z_{\omega_1}$)

QR-DQN

Quantile Regression Deep Q-Network

  • Represents the value distribution by estimating a fixed set of quantiles.
  • Uses quantile regression loss to train the network.
  • Improves stability and expressiveness over categorical methods.
  • Distributional Reinforcement Learning with Quantile Regression, Dabney et al., 2017.
    Algorithm: QR-DQN
QR-DQN approxime la distribution des retours par une série de quantiles, ce qui permet une estimation plus fine et une meilleure robustesse que les approches discrètes classiques.
QR-DQN models the return distribution using quantiles, enabling more precise and robust value estimation than fixed categorical bins.

Extensions

QR with Utility, Risk-sensitive RL

  • Integrate utility functions into quantile-based RL for risk-sensitive objectives.
  • Customize agent behavior: risk-averse, risk-seeking, or robust policies.
  • Applications in finance, robotics, and safety-critical domains.
Les extensions des méthodes quantiles permettent d’intégrer des fonctions d’utilité ou des critères de risque, rendant l’agent sensible à la variance ou à l’asymétrie des retours selon l’application.
Extensions of quantile-based RL incorporate utility or risk criteria, enabling agents to optimize for specific risk preferences or robust performance.

Distributed RL / Scaling RL

A3C

Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic

  • Multiple agents run in parallel, each with its own environment copy.
  • Asynchronous updates to shared parameters.
  • Improves exploration and training speed.
A3C permet une exploration diversifiée et une accélération de l’apprentissage grâce à la parallélisation et à la mise à jour asynchrone des paramètres.
A3C leverages parallelism and asynchronous updates to stabilize and speed up RL training.

Ape-X

Distributed Prioritized Experience Replay

  • Many actors generate experience in parallel.
  • Centralized learner updates the policy using prioritized replay.
  • Scalable to large clusters, efficient data collection.
Ape-X sépare la collecte d’expérience (acteurs) et l’apprentissage (learner), ce qui permet de tirer parti de ressources distribuées à grande échelle.
Ape-X architecture enables scalable RL by decoupling experience generation and learning.

IMPALA

Importance Weighted Actor-Learner Architecture

  • Many actors send trajectories to a central learner.
  • Uses V-trace for off-policy correction.
  • Highly scalable, supports thousands of actors.
IMPALA permet un apprentissage efficace à grande échelle grâce à la correction off-policy (V-trace) et à une architecture centralisée.
IMPALA achieves high throughput and scalability with off-policy corrections and centralized learning.

R2D2

Recurrent Experience Replay in Distributed RL

  • Distributed experience replay with recurrent networks (LSTM).
  • Handles partial observability and long-term dependencies.
  • Combines scalability with memory-based policies.
R2D2 combine la scalabilité du replay distribué avec la capacité des réseaux récurrents à gérer des dépendances temporelles longues.
R2D2 enables distributed RL with recurrent policies, suitable for partially observable environments.
